### **Problem Setup**

* **Mass of the runner ($m_1$):** 70 kg
* **Initial velocity of the runner ($v_{1i}$):** 3 m/s
* **Mass of the cart ($m_2$):** 140 kg
* **Initial velocity of the cart ($v_{2i}$):** 0 m/s (stationary)

---

### **Step 1: Finding the Final Speed (Conservation of Momentum)**

**Explanation and Why We Use It:**
When the runner jumps onto the cart and they begin to move together as a single unit, this is a classic example of a **perfectly inelastic collision**. In any isolated system (assuming we ignore the friction of the cart's wheels against the ground), the total momentum before the interaction must equal the total momentum after the interaction.

**Formulas:**
* Momentum: $p=mv$
* Law of Conservation of Momentum: 
  $$m_1v_{1i} + m_2v_{2i} = (m_1 + m_2)v_f$$

Where $v_f$ is the final combined speed of the runner and the cart.

**Solution:**
Since the cart is initially stationary ($v_{2i} = 0$), the initial momentum of the cart is zero, which cancels out the second term on the left side of the equation. We plug in our given values:

$$70 \cdot 3 + 140 \cdot 0 = (70 + 140)v_f$$
$$210 = 210v_f$$

Now, we solve for the final velocity ($v_f$):

$$v_f = \frac{210}{210}$$
$$v_f = 1 \text{ m/s}$$

---

### **Step 2: Checking Conservation of Kinetic Energy**

**Explanation and Why We Use It:**
The problem asks: "Is kinetic energy conserved in this collision?" To answer this, we need to calculate the total kinetic energy of the system **before** the jump and compare it to the total kinetic energy **after** the jump. 

**Formula:**
* Kinetic Energy: $KE=\frac{1}{2}mv^2$

**Solution:**
1. **Initial Kinetic Energy (Before):**
Only the runner is moving; the cart is stationary and has zero kinetic energy.

$$KE_i = \frac{1}{2}m_1v_{1i}^2$$
$$KE_i = \frac{1}{2} \cdot 70 \cdot 3^2$$
$$KE_i = 35 \cdot 9 = 315 \text{ J (Joules)}$$

2. **Final Kinetic Energy (After):**
Now, the runner and the cart are moving together at 1 m/s, so we add their masses together (210 kg).

$$KE_f = \frac{1}{2}(m_1 + m_2)v_f^2$$
$$KE_f = \frac{1}{2} \cdot 210 \cdot 1^2$$
$$KE_f = 105 \cdot 1 = 105 \text{ J (Joules)}$$

**Conclusion:**
$315 \text{ J} \neq 105 \text{ J}$. Therefore, kinetic energy is **not conserved**. In perfectly inelastic collisions, kinetic energy is never conserved. A portion of the initial energy of motion (in this case, 210 Joules) is "lost"—it is transformed into other forms of energy, such as thermal energy (friction from the runner's shoes hitting the cart), sound waves from the impact, and the deformation of materials.

---

### **Final Answer**

1. The final speed of the cart with the runner is **1 m/s**.
2. **No**, kinetic energy is not conserved in this collision. It decreased from 315 J to 105 J because the missing energy was transformed into heat and sound upon impact.
